THE RUN GAME IN FOOTBALL


As football evolves, so does the way offenses attack defenses. Modern analytics have pushed this forward.

by Matt Germack

San Francisco 49ers head coach Kyle Shanahan is widely regarded as one of the NFL’s most innovative coaches. He hadn’t earned that title following league-wide trends.

The NFL underwent an offensive revolution in the 2010’s. The information era swept the country, and sports were no different — in football’s case, modern analytics show passing the ball is more efficient than running the ball.

Shanahan didn’t buy in, though. In 2019, he had a major problem at quarterback — Jimmy Garoppolo, whose career appears in jeopardy after just six starts outside of Shanahan’s offense. Not able to rely on his passing game, he constructed a run scheme based on giving his running backs two things — favorable boxes to run into and open space to create explosive plays.

It worked. The 49ers ran the ball more than all but one team in the league. Paired with a stifling defense, that attack turned in a 13-3 season and the 49ers made the Super Bowl.

Garoppolo threw the ball just eight times in a 37-20 demolition of the Packers in the NFC Championship game that year, the fewest pass attempts in a playoff win since 1974.


How did we get here?

Running the ball to set up the pass, an ages-old philosophy, was proven false. In turn, many modern offenses turned to their air attacks to set up explosive runs. 15 of the 16 most pass-happy seasons in NFL history have come since 2009 — there’s been 15 seasons since 2009.

And as a result, even as pass rates continue to increase, the five seasons with the highest league-wide yards per carry mark in NFL history are 2018 through 2022.



WHY IS THIS THE CASE?

A major part of this trend is the rise of the run-pass option, or RPO. RPOs are run plays with an option to pass built in — based on how the defense reacts, quarterbacks must either hand the ball off or throw to open space.




Teams are using RPO's more than ever. League-wide, the percentage of plays that were RPOs has steadily increased since 2016, according to a Pro Football Focus analysis. The same analysis said 43% of runs from shotgun formations in 2021 had a pass option attached to it.

RPO-based schemes also take advantage of modern rule changes. RPO's give defenders less time to get in position for the run, and new rules in the name of player safety force defenders to square up.



Defenders are no longer allowed to tackle by the collar, lower their helmet to initiate contact or launch off the ground when tackling.





These adjustments have made it much easier for running backs to operate. The position is famously one of the most tolling on the body — with new rules, backs can pay less attention to protecting themselves.



A great example of this is the Tennessee Titans’ Derrick Henry. The 6-foot-3, 247-pound back is known as one of the league’s hardest players to tackle. But that may not have been the case without rule changes.


He’s known for “running high”; essentially, he doesn’t lower his shoulders or hips much when he runs. This is a disastrous strategy for smaller backs, but for Henry, his natural strength and power makes it a non-difference.

Running high exposes his midsection. If defenders could lower their helmets into it, or launch into him, he’d be at a much higher risk of injury. But defenders can’t. And the extra speed this gives Henry makes him one of the league’s best backs because of it.

We don’t have complete data for the 2023 season yet. But we’re likely to see these trends continue in a year that’s seen Dolphins head coach Mike McDaniel — a former Shanahan assistant — revolutionize how offenses use motion.
